Summary vs. Formal Administration in Florida: Which Applies to Your Case?

By Hector A. Chavez  ·  Florida Probate & Estate Planning Document Preparation

One of the first decisions in any Florida probate matter is determining which administration track applies. Florida law provides two primary options — Summary Administration and Formal Administration — and the difference has a direct impact on the documents required, the timeline, and the complexity of the matter.

What Is Summary Administration?

Summary Administration is a simplified probate process available for estates that meet specific eligibility criteria under Florida Statute §735.201. It is generally faster and requires fewer court filings than Formal Administration, making it the preferred option when it applies.

Eligibility Requirements

  • The decedent has been dead for more than two years, regardless of estate value; or
  • The value of the entire estate subject to administration in Florida does not exceed $75,000
  • The decedent was not a Florida resident (with no Florida assets triggering formal requirements)

Key Documents for Summary Administration

  • Petition for Summary Administration
  • Order of Summary Administration
  • Proof of publication (Notice to Creditors in a local newspaper)
  • Death certificate
  • Original will (if testate)

What Is Formal Administration?

Formal Administration is the standard probate process required for estates that do not qualify for summary administration. It involves appointment of a Personal Representative, creditor notification periods, and court oversight throughout the process.

When Formal Administration Is Required

  • The estate exceeds $75,000 in value and the decedent died within the last two years
  • The decedent had outstanding debts that need to be formally resolved
  • There are disputes among beneficiaries or creditors
  • Real property must be transferred through probate

Key Documents for Formal Administration

  • Petition for Administration
  • Notice of Administration
  • Notice to Creditors
  • Letters of Administration
  • Inventory of estate assets
  • Accounting documents
  • Petition for Discharge (upon closing)

Simple vs. Complex Formal Administration

Not all formal administration cases are alike. A straightforward matter — a modest estate with a clear will, cooperative beneficiaries, and no creditor disputes — can often be resolved efficiently with a standard document set. More complex matters may involve multiple asset types, contested claims, or multi-jurisdictional considerations that require expanded filings and correspondence.

How Document Preparation Support Helps

Whether you are handling a Summary or Formal Administration matter, preparing the document package correctly and completely from the start is critical. Errors in petition language, missing exhibits, or improper formatting can delay court acceptance and extend your timeline. Working with a dedicated document preparation professional ensures your filings are organized, accurate, and ready for submission — so your team can stay focused on the legal strategy and client relationship.

Estate Planning

The 5 Essential Estate Planning Documents Every Florida Client Needs

By Hector A. Chavez  ·  Florida Probate & Estate Planning Document Preparation

A complete Florida estate plan is more than just a will. Attorneys who take the time to ensure their clients have all five core documents in place provide a level of protection that extends well beyond the client's death — covering incapacity, healthcare decisions, and asset transfer in a coordinated, legally sound package.

1. Last Will & Testament

The foundation of any estate plan. A properly executed Florida will designates beneficiaries, names a Personal Representative, and — critically for parents — designates a guardian for minor children. Florida requires the will to be signed in the presence of two witnesses and a notary. Document preparation support ensures the formatting, signature blocks, and execution requirements are set up correctly every time.

2. Durable Power of Attorney

A Florida Durable Power of Attorney (DPOA) under Chapter 709 authorizes an agent to manage financial affairs on the principal's behalf — including banking, real estate transactions, and business decisions. "Durable" means it remains effective even if the principal becomes incapacitated. This is one of the most important documents for clients who want to avoid guardianship proceedings.

3. Designation of Health Care Surrogate

Under Florida Statute §765.203, a Healthcare Surrogate designation authorizes a trusted individual to make medical decisions when the principal cannot. This is distinct from a living will — the surrogate makes real-time decisions that may not be anticipated in advance. Every adult client should have one.

4. Living Will / Advance Directive

A Living Will documents the client's wishes regarding life-prolonging procedures in the event of a terminal condition, end-stage condition, or persistent vegetative state. Florida's statutory form under §765.303 provides a clear structure, but the document should be tailored to the client's specific instructions. Properly drafted, it gives families and medical providers clear guidance and reduces conflict.

5. Declaration of Pre-Need Guardian (Optional but Recommended)

Often overlooked, this document allows a client to designate who they want appointed as their guardian if a court ever determines guardianship is necessary. While a strong DPOA and Healthcare Surrogate designation reduce the likelihood of guardianship proceedings, having a pre-need declaration on file gives the court direction if proceedings become unavoidable.

Why Complete Packages Matter

Clients who come in for "just a will" are often unaware that an incomplete plan can create the exact family conflict and court involvement they want to avoid. Attorneys who consistently deliver complete, well-drafted estate planning packages build lasting client relationships and generate referrals. Document preparation support allows your team to maintain that standard across every matter — without the bottleneck of in-house drafting.

How Small Firms and New Attorneys Can Compete on Probate Volume Without Hiring Staff

By Hector A. Chavez  ·  Florida Probate & Estate Planning Document Preparation

One of the most common challenges for small probate practices and newly barred attorneys is capacity. The demand for Florida probate and estate planning services is consistent — Florida's aging population ensures a steady stream of matters — but building the internal resources to handle that volume efficiently takes time and money that most new practices don't have at the start.

The Staffing Problem

Hiring a full-time legal assistant to handle document preparation comes with significant overhead: salary, benefits, training time, and the risk that the hire simply doesn't work out. For a solo attorney or small firm still building its client base, that commitment is a significant financial risk. And even when a firm does invest in staff, turnover in legal support roles is common — leaving the attorney scrambling to manage document preparation themselves while also handling client matters.

What Document Preparation Outsourcing Actually Looks Like

Outsourcing document preparation to a specialized professional is not about losing control of your work product — it's about delegating the time-consuming drafting tasks so you can stay focused on the legal strategy and client relationships that only you can handle.

The model is straightforward:

  • You handle client intake and consultation. You meet with the client, gather information, and make the legal determinations.
  • You send the intake details to your document preparation professional. A simple intake form or summary is usually sufficient.
  • Documents are drafted, reviewed, and delivered to you — typically within 2–3 business days — ready for your review and client signature.
  • You review, finalize, and file. Your name is on the work. You maintain full control of the matter and the client relationship.

The Economics Make Sense

At a flat rate of $350–$900 per probate case and $300–$550 per estate planning matter, document preparation support costs a fraction of what in-house staff would cost for the same work. For a firm handling 10 probate matters per month, the math is straightforward — and the time recovered by not drafting documents personally translates directly into the ability to take on more clients.

What to Look for in a Document Support Partner

  • Florida-specific experience. Probate and estate planning document requirements vary significantly by state. Your support professional should have direct, hands-on experience with Florida court procedures and Florida Statute requirements.
  • Confidentiality standards. Client information must be handled with the same discretion you would expect of any member of your team. Ask about their data handling practices and whether they will sign an NDA.
  • Clear turnaround commitments. Deadlines matter in probate. Your support professional should be upfront about turnaround times and responsive when you have urgent needs.
  • Quality that requires minimal review. The goal is to reduce your workload, not add a review burden. A strong document preparation professional delivers work that needs little to no correction before submission.

The Bigger Picture

For newly barred attorneys, The Right Draft is designed to be a bridge — a way to handle the document load professionally while your practice grows to the point where hiring staff makes financial sense. For established small firms, it's an efficient way to handle volume spikes and complex matters without the overhead of additional headcount. Either way, the result is the same: more time for the legal work that drives your practice forward.
